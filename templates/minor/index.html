Findings

What actually predicts churn

Eight variables, tested individually against churn rate. Four told a real story — four turned out to be noise. Both are worth knowing.

Strongest signal

The first three months are the danger zone

Customers churn at 41.9% within their first three months — nearly seven times the rate of customers in their second year. Average tenure of churned customers is 3.9 months, versus 11.4 months for retained ones. Whatever causes churn, it happens early, or not at all.

Churn rate by tenure
Churn by tenure: 0-3m 41.9%, 4-6m 7.5%, 7-12m 5.7%, 1-2yr 6.5%, 2yr+ 0%
Strong signal

A complaint nearly triples churn risk

Customers who filed a complaint churned at 31.7%, compared to 10.9% for those who didn't. This is one of the single largest gaps in the dataset — and one of the most actionable, since complaints are logged the moment they happen.

Churn rate by complaint status
Churn by complaint: No complaint 10.9%, Complaint filed 31.7%
Moderate signal

Smaller cities, higher churn

Tier 3 cities churn at 21.4%, against 14.5% in Tier 1. The gradient is consistent — each tier down correlates with higher churn, suggesting service or delivery friction rather than customer preference.

Churn rate by city tier
Churn by city tier: Tier 1 14.5%, Tier 2 19.8%, Tier 3 21.4%
Counter-intuitive

High satisfaction doesn't mean low churn

Customers who rated their satisfaction a 5 churned at 23.8% — the highest of any score, even above a rating of 1 (11.5%). Likely explanation: satisfaction may reflect a single resolved interaction (like a complaint outcome) rather than overall loyalty.

Churn rate by satisfaction score
Churn by satisfaction score: 1: 11.5%, 2: 12.6%, 3: 17.2%, 4: 17.1%, 5: 23.8%

Tested, ruled out

App usage hours

2.93 vs 2.96 hrs avg — no meaningful gap between churned and retained.

Coupons used

1.72 vs 1.71 avg — engagement via coupons doesn't predict loyalty here.

Days since last order

Churned customers ordered more recently on average — likely a labeling artifact, not a behavioral signal.

Cashback amount

₹180.6 vs ₹160.4 avg — a real but modest gap, secondary to tenure and complaints.

Modeling

Four models, one clear winner

Optimized for recall — for a retention team, missing a churning customer is far costlier than a false alarm on a loyal one.

Model Accuracy Recall Precision
Logistic Regression (baseline) 86% 30% 73%
Support Vector Machine 85% 59% 59%
Random Forest 95.7% 75% 100%
XGBoost — selected 97.5% 90% 95%

The selected model catches 171 of 190 churning customers in the test set, with only a handful of false alarms — strong enough to act on directly, not just describe.

Recommendations

Five places to intervene

Each tied to a specific, measurable finding above — framed as hypotheses worth testing, not guaranteed fixes.

01

Redesign the first 90 days

Tenure is the strongest predictor of churn. A milestone-based onboarding sequence — welcome offer, second-order nudge, third-order reward — targets the exact window where customers are most likely to leave.

02

Respond to complaints like they're urgent

A filed complaint nearly triples churn odds. Routing complaints to a fast-response queue, paired with an automatic retention offer, addresses the highest-leverage signal in the dataset.

03

Audit service quality in Tier 2 and 3 cities

The churn gradient across city tiers points to operational friction — likely delivery speed or fulfillment reach — rather than a difference in customer preference.

04

Re-engage one-time electronics buyers

Mobile and electronics shoppers churn over five times more than grocery shoppers, consistent with one-off, comparison-shopped purchases. Post-purchase bundles or extended-warranty offers could extend the relationship.

05

Put the model into monthly use

At 90% recall, the model is accurate enough to score the active customer base monthly, flagging high-risk accounts to the retention team before cancellation — not after.
